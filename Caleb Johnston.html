About Me

I study American Government at Georgetown University because I want to better understand how institutions shape people's lives — and how public service can make those institutions more just, responsive, and humane.

My work sits at the intersection of governance, direct service, political education, and community-centered public action. At Georgetown, I have served as a Graduate Teaching Assistant and worked with the Prisons and Justice Initiative, supporting reentry-focused programming and engaging with the practical challenges people face as they prepare to return to the community.

I earned my B.A. in Government and Economics from the University of Texas at Austin, where I built an interdisciplinary foundation in political institutions, economic analysis, and empirical research.

My interests center on governance, institutional design, democratic accountability, political behavior, and the ways public systems can better serve marginalized communities. Across my work, I try to combine evidence-based analysis with grounded experience — studying institutions carefully while staying close to the human realities that make institutional reform necessary.

The throughline is service: understanding systems, working with people, and trying to help build institutions worthy of the communities they are meant to serve.

Research

Working Paper · Forthcoming July 2026

Reentry, Governance, and the Role of Nongovernmental Organizations

M.A. Capstone · GOVT 7951 · Georgetown University

This paper examines how nongovernmental reentry organizations and their frontline practices mediate the transition from incarceration to community reintegration — and what this reveals about reentry as a problem of governance.

Drawing on scholarship in mass incarceration, prisoner reentry, and public administration, the paper argues that reentry is not simply what happens after punishment ends, but one of the ways punishment can continue to operate. It uses Georgetown University's Prisons and Justice Initiative as a qualitative case study to examine how frontline reentry work either interrupts, softens, or reproduces that continuation of punishment.

The expected contribution is to show that nongovernmental reentry organizations do more than provide services — they help govern the transition from incarceration to community life by interpreting institutional rules, mediating access to resources, and shaping how returning citizens experience reintegration.
